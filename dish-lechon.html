Lechon (Litson)

Whole Pig Roasted Fiesta

What is Lechon?

Lechon is a whole pig roasted over charcoal on a bamboo spit. The skin becomes incredibly crispy and golden brown, while the inside stays juicy and flavorful. It is the king of all Filipino dishes and the centerpiece of every fiesta table.

In Kapampangan, it is called Litson. Kapampangan lechon is known for being stuffed with lemongrass, garlic, onions, and other aromatics that make the meat fragrant and delicious.

How Lechon Is Made

Making lechon is a community activity that takes the whole day. Here is how it is traditionally done:

  1. The pig is cleaned and prepared the night before.
  2. The inside of the pig is stuffed with lemongrass, garlic, onions, leeks, and bay leaves.
  3. The pig is sewn up and mounted on a long bamboo pole.
  4. The pole is suspended over a pit of burning charcoal.
  5. Two people take turns slowly turning the pig for 4 to 6 hours.
  6. The skin is basted with water or coconut water to help it crisp up.
  7. When the skin is deep brown and crackly, the lechon is done.
The Best Part: The skin! Kapampangans fight over the crispy skin of the lechon. It is thin, golden, and shatters like glass when you bite into it. This is achieved through hours of patient slow-roasting.

Lechon Sauce

Kapampangan lechon is traditionally served with sarsa ng litson — a liver-based sauce made from the pig's liver, vinegar, sugar, and breadcrumbs. This thick, tangy sauce balances the richness of the roasted pork perfectly.

Lechon in Fiestas

No Kapampangan fiesta is complete without lechon. When a family orders lechon, it means they are celebrating something big — a birthday, a wedding, a town fiesta, or graduation. It is a symbol of generosity and prosperity.
