Tibok-Tibok

Fading Dessert Carabao Milk Pudding

What is Tibok-Tibok?

Tibok-Tibok is a traditional Kapampangan pudding made from pure carabao milk, rice flour, and sugar. When cooked, it forms a silky, smooth pudding that jiggles and quivers when moved — like a heartbeat. That is where its name comes from: tibok means heartbeat in Filipino.

It is topped with latik — crispy golden coconut curd made by boiling coconut cream until the solids separate and toast. The latik adds a crunchy, nutty contrast to the soft, creamy pudding.

Why Is It Fading Away?

The biggest problem is the milk. Authentic Tibok-Tibok must be made with fresh carabao milk. As carabao farming has declined in Pampanga, fresh carabao milk has become very difficult to find. Some cooks try to make it with cow's milk, but the result is not the same — carabao milk is much richer and creamier.

Making latik also requires time and careful attention. It is easy to burn the coconut cream if you are not watching it carefully. Many young cooks find this too difficult.

Tibok-Tibok Recipe

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups carabao milk (or full-fat cow's milk)
  • 1/2 cup rice flour
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1 cup coconut cream (for latik topping)
  • Pinch of salt

For the Latik:

  • 1 cup coconut cream

Instructions:

  1. Make the latik first: boil coconut cream over medium heat, stirring often, until the oil separates and the solids turn golden brown. Remove from heat and set aside.
  2. Mix rice flour, sugar, and salt with carabao milk in a pot.
  3. Cook over low heat, stirring constantly, until thick and smooth.
  4. Pour into a flat dish or mold. Let cool to room temperature.
  5. Refrigerate for at least 2 hours until fully set.
  6. Top with latik before serving. Slice and serve cold.

Where Can You Still Find It?

Tibok-Tibok can sometimes be found at the market in Macabebe, Pampanga, where some elderly vendors still make and sell it. Some high-end Kapampangan restaurants in Angeles City also serve it. But it is not a common dish anymore.

"When I was young, every grandmother in our neighborhood made Tibok-Tibok for fiestas. Now my own children have never even tasted it." — An elderly Kapampangan woman
