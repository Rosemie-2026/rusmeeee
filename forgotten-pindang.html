Pindang Damulag

Almost Extinct Cured Meat Carabao Pre-Colonial

What is Pindang Damulag?

Pindang Damulag is cured and dried carabao (water buffalo) meat. "Damulag" means carabao in Kapampangan. The meat is salted, seasoned, and then dried under the sun for several days until it becomes very firm and preserved.

It was once a staple food in Kapampangan households that kept carabaos as working animals. When a carabao became too old to work, it was slaughtered and the meat was cured so it could be eaten over several weeks without spoiling.

Why Is It Disappearing?

Carabao farming in Pampanga has declined sharply. Modern farming equipment has replaced the carabao as a working animal. Fewer families keep carabaos, so there is less carabao meat available. Today, pork and chicken dominate Kapampangan cooking, and carabao meat is very rarely eaten.

Cultural Connection: The carabao is the national animal of the Philippines. Traditionally, it was the most valuable animal a farming family could own. Pindang Damulag is a reminder of this deep connection between the Kapampangan people and the carabao.

How Was It Made?

  1. Carabao meat was cut into thick slices or strips.
  2. It was rubbed generously with salt and sometimes garlic.
  3. The seasoned meat was hung or laid flat in the sun to dry.
  4. It dried for 3 to 5 days, depending on the weather.
  5. When fully dried, it was stored in a cool, dry place.
  6. To eat, it was fried in lard or grilled over charcoal until crispy.

What Did It Taste Like?

Pindang Damulag tasted salty and savory, similar to beef jerky but with the stronger, earthier flavor of carabao meat. When fried, it became crispy on the outside and chewy inside. It was eaten with rice and vinegar dipping sauce.
