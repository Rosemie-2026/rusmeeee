Key Ingredients in Kapampangan Cooking

Understanding the ingredients used in Kapampangan cooking helps explain why the food tastes so distinctive. Here are the most important ingredients you will find in a Kapampangan kitchen.

Bagoong Alamang (Fermented Shrimp Paste)

Bagoong Alamang is made from tiny shrimp fermented with salt. It has a very strong, salty smell and a deeply savory, pungent flavor. It is an essential condiment served alongside Kare-Kare and is also used as a cooking ingredient in many Kapampangan dishes.

Calamansi (Philippine Lime)

Calamansi is a small citrus fruit native to the Philippines. It is more sour and fragrant than regular lime. It is used to add acidity to dishes like Sisig and as a dipping sauce base. Squeezing calamansi juice over food brightens all the flavors instantly.

Annatto Seeds (Atsuete)

Annatto seeds are small red seeds used mainly for coloring. When soaked or heated in oil, they release a bright orange-red color. This color is used in Kare-Kare sauce, Kapampangan longganisa, and many other dishes. The flavor is mild and slightly earthy.

Carabao Milk (Gatas ng Kalabaw)

Carabao milk is richer, creamier, and more flavorful than regular cow's milk. It is the essential ingredient in Tibok-Tibok pudding and Pastillas de Leche candy. As carabao farming has declined, this milk has become harder to find, which is one reason traditional Kapampangan desserts are disappearing.

Patis (Fish Sauce)

Patis is a clear, amber-colored liquid made from fermented fish. It is used as a seasoning instead of salt in many Filipino dishes. Kapampangan cooking uses patis to add depth and umami flavor. It has a salty, savory taste with a slightly fishy smell that mellows when cooked.

Sukang Iloko / Sukang Maasim (Cane Vinegar)

Vinegar is one of the oldest and most important ingredients in Kapampangan cooking. It was used to preserve meat before refrigerators existed. Cane vinegar is mild and slightly sweet. It is used in adobo, kinilaw, and various dipping sauces.
